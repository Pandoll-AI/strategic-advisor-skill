# Source Reliability Policy

## Source hierarchy

1. **Primary/authoritative**
   - Laws, regulations, official government/regulator pages
   - Standards bodies
   - Official product documentation and release notes
   - Company filings, official investor relations, direct announcements
   - Peer-reviewed papers, clinical guidelines, systematic reviews
   - Original datasets or databases

2. **High-quality secondary**
   - Reputable news organisations
   - Recognised analysts/research institutions
   - Expert explainers with transparent sources
   - Books/reports from credible organisations

3. **Context-only / low-authority**
   - Blogs, social posts, forums, Reddit, marketing pages, unsourced aggregators
   - Use to discover leads, not to prove critical claims.

## Freshness rules

- Treat current role holders, prices, law, regulation, product specs, software APIs, model capabilities, market numbers, clinical guidance, and company facts as unstable.
- Use absolute dates in findings when freshness matters.
- State access/retrieval date for sources in a research memo.

## Evidence grading

| Grade | Meaning |
|---|---|
| A | Direct primary source, current, highly relevant |
| B | Reliable secondary source or older primary source still likely valid |
| C | Partial/indirect evidence, lower authority, or stale |
| D | Weak, anecdotal, marketing-only, or unsupported |

## Citation discipline

- Link every material factual claim to evidence.
- Cite exact location for long documents: page/table/line/sheet/slide.
- Avoid source laundering: do not cite a source that merely repeats another unverified claim.
- When evidence conflicts, present the conflict.
